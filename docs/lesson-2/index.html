Types of tea

Withering

As soon as a tea leaf is plucked, it begins to wilt. Withering is essentially the process of controlled wilting, which prepares the leaves for processing by reducing their moisture content. The process usually occurs either outside or indoors in troughs with forced air. The withering process is complete once the tea leaves have lost around 30-50% of their moisture, which typically takes 8-20 hours.

The withering process causes the membranes of the tea leaves to open, making them more supple and less prone to breaking in further processing steps. Withering also has chemical effects: the chlorophyll in the leaf begins to disintegrate, caffeine levels increase, new flavor and aroma compounds develop, and grassy flavors dissipate. So, the “grassier” teas, like green tea, are typically withered for a short amount of time, or sometimes not at all.

Oxidizing

Fixing is the process of slightly heating the tea leaves. This procedure is done to stop the oxidation of the leaves, which begins during the withering process as the cells in the leaves are damaged. The heat denatures the oxidative enzymes in the leaf and greatly slows the oxidation process.

A tea that has been fixed retains its green color and vegetal taste. Semi-oxidized tea leaves present with a slight browning, and produce a more yellow liquid with a more complex flavor. Fully oxidized tea leaves will be a dark brown and produce a dark red liquid. Thus, controlling the oxidation of the leaves controls the flavor and chemcial profile of the tea.
